As We May Think -- Vannevar Bush, 1945

The understanding of science has been the essential foundation for mankind’s standard
of living since the distance of time. While this understanding has exponentially
progressed, the potential and objectives of many research fields seemed to peak in war
technologies. However, Dr. Vannevar Bush proposed a new incentive for the application
of science beyond warfare— the “memex.” Bush felt the most orderly way to proceed
with the potential of human intelligence would require access to the sum of knowledge
thus far. The memex would then serve as a personal mechanized library of records and
communications, to efficiently aid the individual user by searching through a sea of data.
If the memex was successfully built, it would bridge the complications regarding
preservation, compression, and accessibility of knowledge.

Long Live the Web -- Tim Berners-Lee, 2010

Since its creation, the Web has been consistently relied upon, more and more heavily
so, into its role today as an indispensable understructure of culture and society. Tim
Berners-Lee believes this is due to its formation from egalitarian principles and its
original role as a public resource. However, Berners-Lee feels that the value of the Web
is being threatened by numerous groups who look to monopolize users for profit, often
at the cost of violating privacy and human rights. Actions may include jeopardizing
universality by exclusively containing user information; ISP snooping to access
undisclosed information; or controlling accessibility to certain sites through bias or
business deals. In Berners-Lee's perspective, the once open standards of the Web are
steadily being closed off. Society needs to desperately defend the open Web as it is the
strongest extension of our human rights.
